Why Companies Sell Stock

The primary reason is to raise capital (money) to fund expansion, innovate new products, pay off debt, and hire talent without taking on bank loans.

Your Protection: Limited Liability

As a shareholder, you are not personally responsible for the company's debts. The most you can lose is the amount you invested in the stock.

The Two Faces of Stock

Not all stock is created equal. Companies issue two main types, Common and Preferred, which offer different balances of risk, reward, and control to investors.

Common Stock

Held by founders, employees, and the public. Offers voting rights and high growth potential, but carries higher risk.

Preferred Stock

Held by investors like VCs. Offers fixed dividends and higher safety, but usually no voting rights.

How You Make (or Lose) Money

There are two primary ways to profit from stocks. Understanding both is key to understanding your potential return on investment.

Path 1: Capital Gains

This is the profit from selling your stock for more than you paid. The chart shows buying a stock at $100 and selling at $150, for a $50 gain per share.

Path 2: Dividends

These are portions of a company's profits paid out to shareholders, usually quarterly. They provide a steady stream of income separate from the stock's price.

The Unavoidable Risk

Remember, stock values can also fall. If you sell for less than your purchase price, you incur a capital loss. In a bankruptcy, a stock's value can fall to zero.

The Journey to the Public Market

How does a private company's stock end up in your brokerage account? It follows a two-stage path from creation to public trading.

Stage 1: The Primary Market

A private company holds an Initial Public Offering (IPO) to sell brand new shares directly to initial investors (like large funds). The money raised goes directly to the company to fund its growth.

Stage 2: The Secondary Market

Those shares are now traded between investors on a stock exchange (like the NYSE or NASDAQ). The company isn't involved in these daily trades; this is where the public buys and sells from each other.

Investor Shorthand: Stock Categories

Investors use these labels to quickly describe a stock's investment profile. These aren't rigid rules, but helpful categories for building a strategy.

üöÄ Growth Stocks

Fast-growing companies (often tech) that reinvest profits to expand. The bet is on future price increases, not current income.

üíé Value Stocks

Stocks that seem undervalued by the market. Investors buy them hoping the price will rise to reflect the company's true worth.

üí∞ Income Stocks

üèÜ Blue-Chip Stocks

The Market Guardian: The SEC

The entire system is overseen by the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC), a government agency created in 1934. Its mission is to protect investors, maintain fair and orderly markets, and ensure companies disclose key financial information to the public, preventing fraud and manipulation.
